\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{What compiled enmeshment cannot do.} At rank 16, compiled enmeshment carries stance direction (4/4 topics), confident voice, parametric knowledge steering, and dilution immunity (100\% at 4K filler tokens), but does \emph{not} carry novel content absent from the host's training data: fabricated statistics and unknown proper nouns do not survive the rank-16 bottleneck. This boundary motivates the three-layer architecture (Appendix~\ref{sec:three_layer}): compiled enmeshment for stance bias, the curated slot for novel specifics, recursive expansion for deep evidence on demand. On the instruct model, M cannot override RLHF-trained stances (A: 7/7) but it does shape voice and sycophancy resistance (\S\ref{sec:sycophancy}). For ALFWorld, the Mind Tree's primary value is curation as structured context; M hooks during generation are not needed.

\textbf{The M attractor problem.} In multi-turn dialogue, compiled M applies a constant perturbation at every token position; M-shaped tokens accumulate in the KV cache and create a positive feedback loop that converges to repetitive identity assertion after 2--3 turns on the base model. The fix --- disabling M during prefill (applying it only during generation) --- breaks the feedback loop. This reveals a general principle for additive modulation: \emph{constant additive perturbation creates positive feedback through autoregressive generation}; separate the perturbation's influence on processing-context (where it compounds) from its influence on token-generation (where it adds constant signal). Full diagnosis and the prefill-off fix in Appendix~\ref{sec:attractor}.

\textbf{Two mechanisms, one bridge.} The agentic experiments (\S\ref{sec:agentic}) reveal that HEXIS supports two distinct intervention mechanisms through the same hidden-state bridge. Mechanism (a) attention modulation: $\phi$ compiles retrieved Mind Tree nodes into Q/V modulation tensors that bias per-token attention --- load-bearing for stance direction, confident voice, sycophancy resistance, user-preference integration. Mechanism (b) knowledge retrieval and context injection: $\phi_R$ projects hidden states into a retrieval space where cosine similarity identifies relevant nodes; the output is XML injected at the prompt tail --- load-bearing for specific factual content (tool names, parameter values, policy rules). Retrieval ($\phi_R$) serves both: for disposition it selects which belief nodes to compile; for agentic tasks it selects which policy rules to inject. A production HEXIS agent uses both simultaneously.

\textbf{Why not just compress a prompt?} Three properties distinguish compiled M from prompt compression. First, \emph{structural dilution immunity}: prompt compression reduces tokens but compressed tokens still compete for attention; compiled M operates outside the attention window entirely (\S\ref{sec:dilution}). Second, \emph{non-inspectability}: an adversary can read the prompt and argue against its content, but compiled M operates on pre-projection hidden states below the model's chain-of-thought (Appendix~\ref{sec:implicit_validation}). Third, \emph{processing change}: M produces measurable attention divergence (JSD = 0.049 between M-states) and suppresses think-mode activation (0/4 under D/F) --- the model does not merely produce different words, it processes its input differently.

\textbf{Unexplored design space.} We validate one point in a six-axis design space (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:design_space}). Additive blending (Level 1) is the simplest; gated blending could solve the multi-turn attractor by learning when to suppress M. Cross-attention (Level 3) could transfer richer content. Rank 16 suffices for 4B; larger models may benefit from adaptive rank allocation.

\textbf{Broader implications.} Enmeshed networks implement a form of architectural individuation: different Mind Trees compiled through different M-states produce agents with different attention biases, argumentative styles, and behavioural stances --- all from the same frozen host. This is \emph{composition} rather than \emph{fine-tuning}: the host's general capabilities are preserved while M adds an attention-shaped lens.
