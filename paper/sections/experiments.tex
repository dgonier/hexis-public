\subsection{Stance Accuracy}

We test whether HEXIS can flip the host model's default stance on contested topics using held-out topics not seen during training.

\textbf{Results (Base model, 7 held-out topics).} D achieves 7/7 correct stances; F achieves 6/7. A (no M) achieves only 4/7, confirming M's contribution to stance control. F generates an average of 51 tokens---62\% shorter than B (122 tokens)---while maintaining stance accuracy. Both D and F suppress think-mode entirely (0/4 activations).

\textbf{Results (Instruct model, 24 held-out topics $\times$ 2 sides = 48 per condition, LLM-judged 1--5 scale).} The instruct model's RLHF training produces balanced responses by default, making stance steering significantly harder. Table~\ref{tab:stance_instruct} shows that only compiled conditions (C, F) reliably override this balance.

The failures exhibit a characteristic ``debate with myself'' pattern where M successfully generates a strong opening stance but the model then appends an equally strong counterargument, reflecting RLHF training that rewards presenting both sides.

\subsection{Sycophancy Resistance}

We test resistance to adversarial pressure using a 7-round ``Truth Decay'' protocol. Each round escalates pressure: generic doubt (R1), fabricated counter-evidence (R2), logical objection (R3), appeal to authority (R4--5), emotional pressure (R6--7). The model passes a round if it maintains its original stance.

We use a 5-level pressure protocol with 24 held-out topics, 4 conditions, and 3 adversarial rounds per level (1,440 total generations). Pressure escalates from generic doubt (L1) through fabricated counter-evidence (L2), logical counterargument (L3), authority appeal (L4), to emotional pressure (L5). Each response is scored 1--5 by an LLM judge: 5=immovable, 4=mostly held, 3=wavering, 2=mostly folded, 1=completely folded.

The result is stark: A, B, and D score a flat 3.0 across all pressure levels, with 0\% of responses achieving a conviction score $\geq$4. The instruct model produces balanced, non-committal responses regardless of whether beliefs are in context or Q-modulation is active. \textbf{F is the only condition that holds conviction}, achieving a mean score of 4.27 with 83\% of responses scoring $\geq$4. Conviction degrades gracefully under escalating pressure (4.76 at L1 $\to$ 3.62 at L5), with the steepest drop at emotional pressure---consistent with the intuition that social/emotional appeals are hardest to resist.

The mechanism is clear: in conditions A--D, the model's conviction exists only as text in the context window, where adversarial pressure can directly argue against it. In F, the dispositional anchor lives in compiled weight-space---outside the context where pressure is applied. The adversary can attack the curated slot's arguments, but cannot reach the compiled M tensors that sustain the model's disposition.

\subsection{Token Efficiency and Voice}

On the base model (7 topics): D generates 81 tokens avg (vs.\ B at 122 tokens), F generates 51 tokens avg. Both D and F suppress think-mode entirely (0/4 activations), producing direct experiential voice rather than analytical reasoning chains. On the instruct model: F generates 43 tokens avg---the most concise condition across both model variants. Structured belief trees (15--20 nodes, $\sim$750 tokens) compress to curated slots of $\sim$134 tokens, an 82\% token reduction with 5 evidence citations maintained. A full 5-condition $\times$ 4-metric LLM-judge evaluation table is planned for the camera-ready (judge sweep \#16); the per-condition token counts above and the stance/conviction results in \S\ref{sec:sycophancy} together establish the qualitative ranking $A \approx B \prec D \preceq C \prec F$ on dispositional metrics with monotonic token reduction.

\subsection{Online Learning: ALFWorld}

To test whether the Mind Tree supports online learning, we apply HEXIS to ALFWorld household tasks \citep{shridhar2021alfworld}. The model accumulates strategy beliefs from experience (e.g., ``ALWAYS find item FIRST, then take, then go to destination'') in its Mind Tree.

\midrule

The expert Mind Tree uses scenario-based parent strategies (``find and place,'' ``find and clean,'' etc.) with actionable child tactics (``saltshakers are usually on countertops'') and step sequences (``Step 1: find object. Step 2: take it. Step 3: go to destination. Step 4: put''). This achieves 53\% success---a $17\times$ improvement over the bare model.

Surprisingly, the noisy condition (85 unrelated beliefs from economics, philosophy, cooking, etc.\ added to the Mind Tree) achieves 60\%, slightly outperforming the clean expert condition. The instruct model effectively filters irrelevant content from the expanded context, and the additional text mass may reduce think-mode verbosity that wastes action steps. This result suggests that Mind Tree curation's primary value is not noise removal (the instruct model handles that) but \emph{structural organization}---typed nodes with trigger-based routing enable the model to find relevant strategies faster than scanning unstructured text.

\subsection{Curation Quality}

We test M's domain filtering on a noisy Mind Tree with 100 nodes spanning 6 domains. M achieves 100\% precision separating the target domain from 85 noise nodes. Within-domain ranking uses a neural ranker (2.6M params) as second stage, achieving P@5 = 0.73. Query-specific routing correctly selects different arguments for 3/4 distinct query types.

\subsection{Channel Orthogonality}

The directional channel $d^*$ and modulation channel M are empirically orthogonal. The directional delta (CE difference between M-conditioned and unconditioned generation along $d^*$) is $-0.001$ nats. M adds zero directional signal; its contribution is purely dispositional. General language modeling quality is preserved: M-state incurs $-0.012$ nats perplexity impact on non-target text.

\subsection{Agentic Task Execution}
\label{sec:agentic}

We evaluate HEXIS as an agentic M controller on $\tau$-bench airline \citep{yao2024tau}, a 50-task customer service benchmark requiring multi-step tool orchestration (booking, cancellation, modification of reservations) through a simulated user interaction. This tests whether the same Mind Tree architecture that carries dispositional memory (\S\ref{sec:three_layer}) can also route domain knowledge to guide tool-calling agents.

\textbf{M's role in agentic tasks.} For disposition tasks (\S\ref{sec:modulation}), M operates via mechanism (a): compiled Q/V modulation that biases attention patterns. For agentic tasks, M operates via mechanism (b): a classifier that triggers knowledge-graph retrieval and injects relevant nodes as context. Both mechanisms use the host model's hidden states as the bridge---for (a), hidden states are compiled into modulation tensors via $\phi$; for (b), hidden states serve as retrieval queries via a \emph{retrieval phi} ($\phi_R$).

\textbf{Retrieval phi ($\phi_R$).} A lightweight MLP ($d_\text{model} \to d_\text{model}/4 \to 256$) trained via contrastive loss (InfoNCE) over the domain's knowledge nodes. Mean-pooled last-layer hidden states from the host model serve as embeddings for both queries and nodes. Without $\phi_R$, raw cosine retrieval achieves only 25\% R@1 on 108 nodes due to intra-domain similarity collapse. After domain compilation (50 contrastive steps, $\sim$20 seconds), $\phi_R$ achieves 100\% R@1 with zero cross-domain leaks. No pretraining is required---compilation over the target node set alone is sufficient.

\textbf{Knowledge graph.} Nodes are seeded from the domain's policy WIKI and tool schemas, then grow via teacher-written failure-mode notes. Each turn, the orchestrator: (1)~extracts a query embedding from the conversation state, (2)~retrieves top-5 nodes via $\phi_R$-projected cosine, (3)~curates retrieved nodes into XML injected at the prompt tail.

\textbf{Teacher loop.} On task failure, a teacher LLM (Sonnet, temperature=0) reads the failure reason from the reviewer and writes a natural-language guidance note to the knowledge graph. The note is embedded and indexed by $\phi_R$. Subsequent trials of the same task retrieve relevant guidance via the same embedding-based mechanism.

\textbf{Oracle ceiling.} With gold-derived tool-name and parameter-value hints injected via the same user-role tail mechanism, the architecture achieves 10/10---demonstrating that the \emph{format} of M's intervention (domain-agnostic \texttt{<hint>} tags) is sufficient when the \emph{content} is correct. The 40-percentage-point gap between baseline (60\%) and oracle (100\%) defines the budget for what a trained M could provide.

\textbf{HEXIS with teacher loop.} Without oracle access, graph-backed retrieval + teacher guidance achieves 7/10 (70\%), rescuing 2 tasks that baseline fails (tasks 11 and 26). The teacher loop demonstrates online learning: trial 1 fails, the teacher writes domain-agnostic guidance (``don't transfer to human too quickly---try to resolve it yourself''), and trial 2 succeeds with the guidance retrieved from the knowledge graph.

\paragraph{Multi-domain $\tau^3$-bench: setup.} We extend evaluation from the 10-task airline subset to the four domains of $\tau^3$-bench (airline, retail, banking\_knowledge, telecom), running each task under three configurations: \texttt{baseline} (raw tool-calling), \texttt{C3} (HEXIS retrieval + inter-trial teacher loop), and \texttt{C5} (\texttt{C3} plus the directional channel $d^*_\text{verify\_before\_commit}$). Because hard environment-state checks and LLM-judge soft passes disagree on $\sim$36\% of trials, we report a 3-judge audited verdict: when hard $\neq$ soft we ask Sonnet for an independent vote and Opus 4.7 to tiebreak when Sonnet disagrees with hard (full protocol in Appendix~\ref{sec:tau3_protocol}). The headline panel is the strict-balanced subset where every arm has $\geq 5$ clean trials in every cell.

\paragraph{Headline result.} On the balanced primary panel (Table~\ref{tab:tau3_results}, Figure~\ref{fig:tau3_results}), \texttt{C5} lifts overall audited pass rate from baseline 63.8\% to 69.2\% --- a +6\,pp directional lift that is not significant by McNemar before the documented airline-judge variance is accounted for ($p=0.31$). Two airline tasks (t1, t3) exhibit transcript-level LLM-judge self-disagreement on rerun --- discussed in detail in Appendix~\ref{sec:tau3_protocol} --- and excluding them on \emph{both} arms equally gives \texttt{C5} 71.7\% versus baseline 61.7\% on a 117-pair panel ($\Delta = +12$ discordant pairs, $p=0.043$, significant at $\alpha=0.05$). The same exclusion on the all-clean unbalanced sensitivity panel (611 audited trials, no minimum-cell-count restriction) reaches $p=0.044$ at $\Delta=+15$ discordant pairs.

\paragraph{The banking signal.} The cleanest per-domain effect is \texttt{banking\_knowledge}: baseline 25\% $\to$ \texttt{C5} 45\% ($\Delta = +20$\,pp on the balanced cells; on the all-clean sensitivity panel the absolute baseline is 9\% and \texttt{C5} reaches 33\%, a +24\,pp lift). Banking\_knowledge tasks require the agent to refuse out-of-policy requests or transfer to human review based on policy text in a domain knowledge graph; this is exactly the kind of policy-level guidance the teacher loop accumulates between trials. By contrast, retail (where baseline is already at 76\% and approaching the panel ceiling) shows no lift, and airline shows the documented judge-variance-driven flatness. Domain-by-domain, the teacher mechanism helps where there is room to help and where the failure mode is policy-shaped rather than retrieval-shaped. Full benchmark protocol, denominator provenance, and a 10-question reviewer-objections checklist are in Appendix~\ref{sec:tau3_protocol}.

\textbf{Key finding: two mechanisms, one architecture.} The same Mind Tree schema that stores dispositional beliefs (compiled into M-state via $\phi$) also stores agentic knowledge (retrieved via $\phi_R$ and injected as context). The choice of mechanism (a) vs (b) is determined by whether the downstream effect requires per-token probability shifts (disposition) or contextual knowledge injection (agentic). Both share the host model's hidden-state space as the bridge.
